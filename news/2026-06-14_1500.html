C3Google Research: A Low-Carbon Computing Platform Built from Retired Smartphones

📰 Article (English)

Researchers at UC San Diego, in collaboration with Google, have developed a sustainable computing platform by repurposing retired smartphones as server infrastructure. Rather than manufacturing new servers, the team extracts motherboards from used phones, replaces Android with a general-purpose Linux distribution, and clusters them using Kubernetes to function as cloud-equivalent compute units. The key insight is that modern smartphone SoCs deliver single-threaded performance comparable to or better than conventional multicore server CPUs, while their manufacturing emissions — once the device is already retired — are essentially sunk costs. By removing non-essential components (displays, batteries, chassis) and clustering 25–50 phones per logical server, the team achieves significant reductions in both operational carbon (via efficiency) and embodied carbon (avoiding new hardware manufacturing). A 20-phone pilot cluster successfully handled peak submission loads for a 75+ student university computing course with sub-second grading latencies. The full deployment targets 2,000 phones providing compute equivalent to roughly 50 traditional servers. Motherboards account for approximately 50% of a smartphone's embodied carbon, making reuse at this component level especially impactful for sustainability goals in data center operations.
